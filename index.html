ⓘ DSM-5 uses "presentation" (not "subtype") to acknowledge that symptom profiles can shift over development. Severity (Mild / Moderate / Severe) is coded separately. Partial remission specifier available if full criteria not currently met.

Clinical note: DSM-5 ADHD diagnosis requires clinical judgment and multi-informant information gathering (e.g., parent + teacher ratings, clinical interview). Rating scales such as the Conners' Parent Rating Scale (CPRS) complement but do not replace clinical assessment. Rule out vision/hearing problems, anxiety, depression, learning disorders, and intellectual disability before diagnosing ADHD.
Pharmacological Treatments
💊
Psychostimulants
Methylphenidate (Ritalin) · Dexedrine · Adderall
▼
First-Line

Psychostimulants increase dopamine and norepinephrine availability in synaptic clefts by blocking reuptake transporters. They address neurobiological deficits in prefrontal attentional circuits. Effective in approximately 75% of children.

  • Short-acting: effects last 3–4 hours; often taken morning ± midday
  • Extended-release formulations available
  • Typically not taken on weekends/holidays ("drug holidays")
  • Produces immediate, noticeable behavioural improvement short-term
  • 8-year MTA follow-up: no long-term benefit demonstrated
⚠ The "paradoxical effect" myth — psychostimulants produce identical effects in neurotypical children and adults (NIMH research). Effect is universal, not ADHD-specific.
⚠️
Side Effects of Stimulants
Growth suppression · Appetite · Sleep
▼
Monitor Closely
  • Decreased appetite — contributes to growth suppression
  • Growth suppression — MTA: 6 lbs less weight + 0.8 inches less height over 3 years
  • Sleeping difficulties / sleep-onset delay
  • Increased heart rate / mild cardiovascular effects
  • Motor tics in a small percentage of children
  • Abuse potential (especially adolescents); up to 35% of college students misuse
Drug holidays (weekends/school breaks) recommended to allow growth recovery. Weigh benefits of symptom control against physical growth costs.
🔵
Strattera (Atomoxetine)
Non-stimulant · Norepinephrine Reuptake Inhibitor
▼
Second-Line

Only FDA-approved non-stimulant for ADHD. Preferred when stimulant abuse is a concern (adults, adolescents, history of substance use).

  • Less effective than psychostimulants overall
  • Lower abuse potential — important in university settings
  • Serious risk of increased suicidal ideation
  • Clonidine (blood pressure med): used adjunctively; reduces aggression; rare sudden death reports
  • Antidepressants: second-line only when stimulants have failed

📊 MTA Study — Multimodal Treatment of ADHD (n = 579)

Largest ADHD treatment RCT. Children randomly assigned to 4 conditions; assessed at 14 months and 8-year follow-up.

Condition 1Controlled medication management
Condition 2Intensive behaviour therapy
Condition 3Combined (med + behaviour)
Condition 4Community care (control)
  • 14 months: Controlled medication and combined treatment superior to behaviour therapy alone and community care
  • Intensive behaviour therapy alone: only marginal benefit over medication
  • Community care (even with unmonitored medication): significantly less effective — medication without monitoring is insufficient
  • 8-year follow-up: No long-term benefit of either treatment — a major unresolved puzzle in ADHD research
Psychosocial Treatments
🎓
Behaviour Therapy
Selective reinforcement · Time-out · Classroom management
▼
Psychosocial
  • Reinforce on-task behaviours: pausing before task, appropriate peer communication
  • Time-out from positive reinforcement: 5–15 min in quiet chair removes attention as reinforcer
  • Reduces aggressiveness, non-compliance, and property destruction
  • Selective reinforcement facilitates academic achievement
  • Most effective when combined with reinforcement of incompatible prosocial behaviours
  • MTA: less effective than medication alone short-term; may have advantages in some domains long-term
👨‍👩‍👧
Behavioural Family Therapy (BFT)
Parent training · Star charts · Daily report cards
▼
Psychosocial

Teaches parents to: specify behavioural expectations clearly, monitor closely, systematically reward positive behaviour, and ignore/mildly punish misbehaviour.

  • Parent training: identify specific problem behaviours, set consistent consequences
  • Tools: star charts, daily report cards coordinating home-school discipline
  • More effective for ODD than for ADHD specifically (MTA)
  • Long-term beneficial effects on antisocial behaviour
BFT targets the reinforcement environment. ADHD has a neurobiological basis that BFT cannot directly address — hence it is adjunctive for ADHD but primary for ODD.
🏛️
Family Interventions
Systemic · Functional Family Therapy (FFT)
▼
Psychosocial
  • Systemic family therapy: focuses on inappropriate family structures, communication patterns, parent-child boundaries (Minuchin)
  • Parent management training: teaches consistent reinforcement of acceptable behaviour; effective for conduct disorder
  • Functional Family Therapy (FFT): views problem behaviour as serving a regulatory function in the family; combines systemic therapy + CBT
  • Meta-analyses: positive effect over no treatment; methodological limitations prevent definitive conclusions
🧠
CBT for ADHD-Related Problems
Adolescents · Secondary depression/anxiety · Comorbidity
▼
Psychosocial

Primarily used with adolescents (not young children — requires cognitive capacity to identify thoughts). Targets secondary consequences: depression and anxiety from chronic academic failure and peer rejection.

  • Identifies pessimistic/negative automatic thoughts and self-blaming attributional patterns
  • Teaches client to challenge and replace dysfunctional cognitions with realistic ones
  • Increases engagement in positively reinforcing behaviours
  • Develops problem-solving, social interaction, and goal-setting skills
  • Parental involvement improves efficacy
CBT addresses cognitive mediators of secondary depression/anxiety. Complements medication that targets primary ADHD symptoms.
Evidence-based recommendation (Davey, 2021): Combined treatment (controlled medication + behaviour therapy) produces the best overall outcomes. Medication alone produces strong short-term symptom reduction but no demonstrated long-term benefit. Psychostimulants remain first-line for core ADHD symptoms; psychosocial interventions are essential for comorbidities, family functioning, and long-term skill development. The absence of long-term medication benefit highlights the need for continued research and multimodal approaches.

A variety of tests can differentiate children with ADHD from control participants. Most assess sustained attention or the ability to inhibit responses. These are used as part of comprehensive neuropsychological assessment; no single test alone is diagnostic (see Seidman, 2006).

Neuropsychological Assessment Tools
CPT
Continuous Performance Test
Computerised vigilance / sustained attention
▼

A computerised visual vigilance/attention task in which the child observes a string of letters presented randomly at varying speeds. Children must press the space bar following all letters except the letter X. Children with ADHD are less able to inhibit responses to the target letter X and show longer reaction times to letters that should be responded to (Epstein, Johnson, Varia & Conners, 2001).

ADHD finding: ↑ commission errors · ↑ reaction time · ↓ response inhibition
Stroop
Stroop Task
Response inhibition · Cognitive interference
▼

A test of the ability to inhibit prepotent responses. A colour word (e.g., RED) is printed in a different ink colour (e.g., green). The participant must name the ink colour while ignoring the word meaning. Children with ADHD take more time to respond and make more errors than control participants (Shin, 2005).

ADHD finding: ↑ response time · ↑ error rate · ↓ inhibitory control
TMT
Trail Making Test
Executive functioning · Cognitive flexibility
▼

A measure involving connecting circles on a page (Reitan, 1958). The child connects circles by drawing lines alternating between circles labelled with numbers and letters in sequential order until reaching the circle labelled 'End'. Most studies show that children and adults with ADHD perform significantly worse than controls (Rapport, Van Voorhis, Tzelepis & Friedman, 2001).

ADHD finding: ↓ completion speed · ↑ errors · ↓ set-shifting ability
COWAT
Controlled Word Association Test
Verbal fluency · Phonological & category fluency
▼

Measures verbal fluency in response to single letters, tapping into phonological associations and category fluency ("name all the animals you can beginning with the letter…"). Assesses speed of access to words, persistence at a task, and processing speed. The majority of studies show impaired performance in children with ADHD compared with controls (Dinn, Robbins & Harris, 2001; Benton, Hamsher & Sivan, 1983).

ADHD finding: ↓ word output · ↓ persistence · ↓ processing speed
CPRS
Conners' Parent Rating Scale
Standardised parent-report · 80 items · 4-point scale
▼

An 80-item scale completed by the child's parent using a four-point rating scale (Conners, Sitarenios, Parker & Epstein, 1998). Well-accepted reliability and validity; considered standard in ADHD diagnosis (Barkley, 1991). Norms by age are available for males and females in 3-year intervals.

Clinical standard · Multi-informant · Normed for age and sex
Executive Functioning Framework

ADHD is fundamentally associated with executive functioning (EF) deficits — the set of cognitive processes regulating goal-directed behaviour. EF deficits are neurologically linked to reduced frontal lobe volume and delayed cortical maturation.

Response Inhibition
Core EF deficit — inability to suppress prepotent, automatic, or impulsive responses
Working Memory
Difficulty holding information in mind while performing other cognitive operations
Cognitive Flexibility
Impaired set-shifting; difficulty adapting responses when task demands change
Assessment note: No single cognitive test is sufficient for ADHD diagnosis. A comprehensive assessment combines: clinical interview, multi-informant rating scales (CPRS + teacher ratings), neuropsychological testing (CPT, Stroop, TMT), direct observation, developmental history, and medical evaluation. Differential diagnosis must exclude anxiety disorders, mood disorders, learning disabilities, sleep disorders, and giftedness.
